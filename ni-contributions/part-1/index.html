§1 — Background

The Letter

Not long ago I received a letter from HM Revenue & Customs. It concerned my Class 3 voluntary National Insurance contributions — the mechanism by which UK citizens living abroad can continue to build entitlement toward the UK State Pension. The letter was polite but direct: "pay the amount due as soon as possible."

Due to new rules which came into effect in April 2026, the amount due was substantially larger than what I had paid in previous years. The instruction was clear. But how could I possibly pay before performing a thorough analysis of the costs and benefits? Besides, effective democracy relies on questioning government — was it really best to pay as soon as possible, or might it be better to defer payment until later?

Without further delay, I decided to bypass my democratically elected government and turn to our AI overlords for advice. Here is what they had to say.

"It was too good to last." — On the abolition of Class 2 voluntary contributions for those living abroad, previously available at a fraction of the Class 3 rate. Those who were paying Class 2 must now switch to Class 3; transitional arrangements ease the eligibility criteria for existing payers but do not protect them from the higher rate.

What This Analysis Is About

The UK State Pension pays a full amount of approximately £12,548 per year to those who accumulate 35 qualifying NI years. Each qualifying year adds roughly 1/35th of the full pension — around £360 per year in retirement income, for life, triple-lock protected, and payable abroad without freezing in the EU and a number of other countries. Note that freezing applies in most Commonwealth countries including Australia, Canada and New Zealand (see GOV.UK for the full list). At the current Class 3 rate of £923 per year, each voluntary contribution pays for itself within roughly three years of drawing the pension.

This analysis does not ask whether to secure a full UK State Pension or try to justify that decision — we take that decision as our starting point. The question is narrower: given that you will eventually make all k remaining NI contributions needed to reach the 35-year threshold, in which years should you make them?

We compare two extreme strategies. Both assume a total annual investment budget of at least the voluntary contribution amount. In any given year, the decision becomes whether to make the voluntary contribution or pay the same amount into some other reliable long-term investment vehicle — to be concrete, we consider a low-cost globally diversified index ETF.

Front-loaded: pay NI contributions in the first k years, then invest the equivalent annual amount in a low-cost ETF for the remaining years.

Back-loaded: invest the annual equivalent in an ETF first, then pay NI contributions in the final k years immediately before retirement.

Between these extremes lies a family of mixed strategies, which we will also explore.

To compare these strategies, we need only consider the pot size of the ETF. This is because the value of the state pension is unaffected by the schedule with which NI contributions are made. We evaluate the ETF pot size at the point of retirement — defined as the year from which the state pension can start being collected. For simplicity, we do not consider the possibility of backpayment, or the possibility of withdrawing from the ETF in order to fund contributions. See below for a discussion of other limitations.

Important: This is a simplified analytical framework, not personalised financial advice. Tax treatment, pension interactions, currency risk, and individual circumstances vary. Consult a qualified adviser before making decisions. See also the disclaimer at the foot of this page.

Chart 1 shows how the two ETF pots evolve year by year. The front-loaded strategy (blue) invests in the ETF only after all NI contributions are complete — its pot begins growing later. The back-loaded strategy (green) invests from the outset and only stops investing to make NI contributions in the final years right before retirement. Backloading dominates: For any positive real return the back-loaded strategy produces a strictly larger pot at retirement, i.e. it dominates the frontloading strategy. Only in the trivially degenerate edge cases — when the number of contribution years k, is either zero or equal to the entire period to retirement T — do the two strategies yield the same pot (which is either empty or the full pot from investing only in the ETF). The advantage must be maximal somewhere in the middle.

At default settings — 5% real return, 30 years, 14 NI years remaining — the difference between back- and front-loading amounts to around £21,000 in real terms. Not a vast amount — roughly the price of a small family hatchback — but practical given the increasing financial pressures to delay parenthood until later in life.

An unexpected symmetry — and its geometric meaning

Chart 4 — Difference in pot size vs NI years remaining

B − A (exact, £)

Chart 4 shows the back-minus-front advantage as a function of NI contribution years remaining k. The curve is perfectly symmetric, peaking at exactly k = T/2, regardless of the ETF return. Where does this symmetry come from?

Each of the T years in the horizon must be assigned to exactly one of two activities: paying NI contributions, or investing in the ETF. Frontloading assigns years 1 through k to NI and the remaining T−k years to ETF. Backloading does the reverse. At k = T/2, frontloading's k NI years are exactly backloading's k ETF years, and vice versa — every single year is assigned to the opposite activity with zero overlap. For k < T/2, some years are ETF years under both strategies (ETF overlap, reducing the advantage). For k > T/2, some years are NI years under both (NI overlap, also reducing the advantage). The midpoint is therefore the point of maximum disagreement between the strategies, making it the point of maximum backloading advantage.

This is a pure counting argument, independent of r. The rate determines the size of the advantage at the peak, but the location k* = T/2 is fixed by the geometry of the time allocation alone.

Try switching the y-axis toggle to the normalised view. This shows the same result as a fraction of the pure ETF pot that would be gained from investing every year in an ETF — the same symmetric bell shape, scaled to [0,1]. How much of the maximum possible ETF return does the timing advantage represent? A ratio of 1 would mean the advantage equals the entire pure ETF pot — unattainable for finite T and r, but approached as r → ∞.

Symmetry: mathematically satisfying, but financially unactionable

Is it all relative?

For practical decision making we often tend to focus on the absolute difference between strategies. However, if you are making lots of similar decisions over your lifetime, it is the relative differences that start to add up. When we consider the ratio, rather than the difference, between front- and back-loading strategies there is in fact a natural and simple result that emerges: $B/A = (1+r)^k$. It states that our final backloading pot is equal to the final frontloading pot compounded with return $r$ for a number of years $k$ equal to the number of contribution years remaining. This is a constant multiplicative factor independent of the number of years until retirement.

Same difference

We can use this result to express the back-minus-front advantage as a fraction of the back-loaded pot itself:

Fractional advantage of back-loading
$$\frac{B - A}{B} = 1 - (1+r)^{-k}$$

This takes a similarly simple form which is also exact and independent of the time horizon $T$. We plot this curve in the chart below:

Unlike the absolute advantage, which peaks at $k=T/2$, the relative advantage increases monotonically with the number of contribution years $k$ and, for all practical intents and purposes, is maximized when contributions need to be made in all but one of the remaining $T$ years until retirement.

Whether we are talking relatively or in absolute terms, higher returns always favour backloading.

The more contributions you have left to make, the more backloading dominates relative to frontloading. Of course, if you have to make contributions for all of your remaining years the absolute difference won't be very large.

On the magnitude of things

We have established that backloading dominates frontloading. To put the advantage in concrete terms it helps to compare it against something familiar: the annual state pension income of £12,548. At the default settings (5% real return, 30 years, 14 NI years remaining) the backloading advantage amounts to roughly one to two years of state pension income at retirement. That may not sound transformative, but it is income the pension itself cannot give you, and — crucially — the ETF pot is not a one-time payment. If it remains invested and continues compounding after retirement, its implied annual income grows with every year. Is this worth playing around with?

The relative advantage that backloading holds over frontloading is independent of the size of the contribution fee. Even for someone still paying Class 2 contributions at the lower rate, backloading beats frontloading by the same relative amount. The difference is only in the magnitude of the sums invested and the magnitude of the backloading advantage received. If the contributions are very small compared to your expected income at retirement (from the state pension and other investments) it is hardly worth playing around with the scheduling.

As things stand, the current Class 3 fees create (hopefully) only a modest backloading advantage compared to your total expected income at retirement. Nevertheless, this modest amount might already tempt some people to consider backloading their contributions. If real-term contributions were to rise more significantly, frontloading would leave more money on the table which could entice more people to play.

Let this serve as a warning to government: increase fees further and we're backloading our payments! *begins frontloading payments in anticipation of government retribution*

Mixed strategies

So far we have only explored extreme strategies in which NI contributions are either fully front- or back-loaded. We have seen that backloading dominates frontloading, and since these different strategies provide the longest and shortest possible periods for compounding investments we can also infer that they are in fact the optimal and worst strategies for maximizing the size of the ETF pot. We now turn to exploring the near-continuum of intermediate strategies that fall somewhere between these two extremes. This will allow us to determine mixed scheduling strategies to match different profiles of risk tolerance and aversion.

We now have a practical single-parameter method for tailoring schedules towards front- or back-loading to suit individual risk preference. Before we commit, however, we should give some more thought to what exactly are the risks?

The trade-off: pension-side versus contribution-side risk

The analysis so far has treated the timing decision as a pure optimisation problem for the size of the investment pot — and in that case back-loading clearly wins. When we start to consider risk it is easy to fall into the trap of focusing only on the risks associated with moving towards this strategy. In reality, both strategies carry qualitatively different risks that point in opposite directions such that no single strategy dominates simultaneously on all dimensions.

Front-loading is exposed to pension-side risk: you pay now, but the retirement age may rise further, the benefit amount may be reduced, cross-country treaty terms may worsen, or means-testing may be introduced.

Back-loading is exposed to contribution-side risk: you plan to pay later, but contribution rates may rise further, the number of qualifying years required may increase, eligibility rules may tighten, or your financial circumstances may not permit the planned payments.

These risks are genuinely asymmetric — moving toward back-loading does not simply increase risk, it trades pension-side risk for contribution-side risk. The timing frontier diagram below captures this: every strategy between A and B is available, and the choice of where to sit is a question of personal risk preference.

Accounting for asymmetric risk

The single parameter families in Chart 7 interpolate smoothly between front- and back-loaded strategies. However, a reader who fears both pension- and contribution-side risks simultaneously, may want to lean towards a mix of both front- and back-loading. This requires a bimodal prior — one that places weight near both year $1$ and year $T$, with sparsity in the middle. The generalised $\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ distribution, with both $\alpha < 1$ and $\beta < 1$, can produce such a density. Note that this is a generalization of our previous single-parameter models — power law and beta quantile schedules — which used the monotonic $\mathrm{Beta}(p, 1)$ and $\mathrm{Beta}(1, p)$ families respectively.

Time inconsistency: planning versus adapting

Perhaps you now have quantified your tolerance to different risks and identified a schedule to follow. You may confidently execute this plan for the first year. Ideally you would also stick to it. But what if you are tempted to re-evaluate your plan again next year, redrawing the optimal schedule for your remaining payments and horizon? This section shows that even a carefully constructed hedging schedule unravels under dynamic re-optimisation — a phenomenon known as time inconsistency.

The time inconsistency trap

Suppose the reader re-evaluates their schedule every year. They fear both pension-side risk (the scheme may deteriorate, favouring deferral) and contribution-side risk (their finances may worsen, favouring early payment) and are uncertain about the timing of either. This motivates a U-shaped $\mathrm{Beta}(0.3, 0.3)$ schedule: paying some contributions early while they can still afford to, and deferring some in case the scheme becomes less favourable. At year 1 they draw this schedule and it calls for an NI contribution immediately, which they pay. At year 2 they consider that the risks haven't materially changed and draw a new $\mathrm{Beta}(0.3, 0.3)$ schedule for the remaining $k-1$ payments over $T-1$ years — and again it calls for an immediate payment. This repeats in years 3 and 4. Despite planning a balanced bimodal schedule at each step, four of the six payments end up being made in the first four years.

Note that while we have used a U-shaped schedule to illustrate the problem, the same inconsistency also affects the monomodal families — a moderately front-loaded schedule drifts further toward front-loading as the near-end anchor is repeatedly executed; a moderately back-loaded schedule drifts further toward back-loading as payments recede towards the far-end anchor.

This is time inconsistency: the schedule drawn at time zero is only coherent as a commitment, followed regardless of what annual re-evaluation would recommend. Without a commitment mechanism, dynamic re-optimisation unravels any intended hedging structure. Nevertheless, we expect circumstances to change — government announcements, changes in employment, budget statements. This is what our risk model tries to capture. As the years go by and we are presented with important new information, how can we make sure to adapt to it in a self-consistent manner?

The natural framework is to use a decision process in which we update our beliefs as new information arrives. The key conceptual shift is from committing to a schedule to committing to a decision rule: a policy that specifies, at each year, whether to pay or defer given the current state and beliefs. Such a policy should be time-consistent by construction and adapt to new information without unravelling. The design of such policies will be addressed in a follow-up.

Don't worry: the 6-year backpayment window sets a hard deadline for how involved the mathematics can get.

Was it time to pay?

Having derived a number of results, the question naturally arose: was I ready to act on the government's instruction? Whether these results were sufficient to reach a decision is left as an exercise for the author. What the analysis does suggest clearly is that the decision is not trivial, that the timing matters, and that — subject to a complex interaction of asymmetric risks — a back-loaded or mixed schedule is likely to outperform paying everything immediately.

§3 — Limitations & Extensions

What the Model Does Not Capture

Annual investment timing

The model treats ETF investments as end-of-year lump sums — a standard ordinary annuity assumption. In practice, investments can be made monthly or at any other frequency, which would slightly alter the compounding dynamics. For the purposes of this analysis the annuity formula is a reasonable and conventional simplification, and the qualitative conclusions are unaffected by this choice.

Taxation

The model ignores taxation on both sides. In the UK, ISA allowances can shelter ETF gains. In Austria, the Kapitalertragsteuer (KESt) levies 27.5% on capital gains, which materially reduces the effective real ETF return. UK pension income is also taxable above the personal allowance. The net effect generally reduces the back-loading advantage, since ETF gains are more heavily taxed in Austria than pension income. A second state pension in GBP also acts as a partial currency hedge — an advantage the ETF alternative does not provide.

Sequence of returns risk

The model assumes a fixed real ETF return each year. In practice, returns are volatile. The back-loaded strategy builds a larger ETF pot earlier — a poor sequence of returns in those early years could significantly underperform the deterministic model. A full stochastic treatment would require Monte Carlo simulation and is beyond the scope of this analysis.

State pension age risk

The UK retirement age is not fixed. Chart 3 directly models this sensitivity: a higher retirement age increases T and amplifies the back-loading advantage. However, if the government raises the retirement age after you have already front-loaded contributions, those years are not wasted — they simply take longer to pay back.

Back-payment windows

The model assumes each contribution pays for exactly one specific tax year, made in real time. This ignores the six-year rolling back-payment window, under which gaps can be filled retrospectively. A more sophisticated analysis would model back-payment strategies explicitly — for example, withdrawing accumulated ETF funds to backfill up to six gap years in a single transaction, or always back-paying the oldest available year to maintain maximum flexibility. Such strategies would likely outperform the back-loaded extreme and the current model's estimates, since they allow the ETF to compound longer before any outflow. Note that the back-payment window also introduces regulatory fragility: if future rule changes shorten or eliminate this window, a pure back-loading approach becomes riskier.

Future rule changes

The April 2026 changes demonstrate that the rules can and do change materially. The direction of recent change — higher costs, stricter eligibility, reduced flexibility — represents contribution-side risk materialising: before these changes it was best to frontload, but since the fees have risen the increased backloading advantage strengthens the case for deferral. Simultaneously, the tightening of eligibility rules increases pension-side risk: the value of future contributions is less certain, which also favours deferral until the rules stabilise. If you are already registered in the system, maintaining that registration actively is valuable regardless of your scheduling strategy.

Future rule changes will probably be announced well in advance — as they were in April 2026 — which is reassuring, unless the pension system itself becomes the subject of a more structural reform, at which point all bets are off.

Early retirement and the back-loading assumption

The model assumes that contributions are funded from ongoing income — that in each year there is a surplus from which either an NI contribution or an ETF investment can be made. This implicitly assumes continued employment until state pension age. For those targeting early retirement, the back-loaded strategy would instead involve funding later contributions from accumulated savings rather than from income. In this case the opportunity cost of a contribution is a liquidation of existing wealth rather than a foregone investment, which reduces the back-loading advantage relative to what the model suggests. The current results are therefore best interpreted as an upper bound on the back-loading advantage for early retirees, with the true advantage depending on how early retirement occurs relative to the planned contribution schedule.

The ETF liquidation scenario

The model assumes contributions are funded from income rather than from the ETF pot. In practice, someone pursuing a back-loaded strategy may find themselves needing to liquidate ETF holdings to fund a given year's contribution. This introduces market timing considerations: redeeming at elevated valuations may represent an opportune moment to sell, while redeeming at a trough could crystallise losses. This interaction is not modelled here but is practically important.

Equations and Reference

Future value of an annuity

A pure ETF strategy investing an amount $C$ every year for all $T$ years with no NI contributions accumulates according to a geometric sum whose solution is the future value of an annuity formula:

Eq. 1 — Pure ETF pot (no NI contributions)
$$\text{Pure ETF} = C\sum_{y=0}^{T-1}(1+r)^y = \frac{C}{r}\left[(1+r)^T - 1\right]$$

In the limit $r \to 0$ this yields (by L'Hôpital's rule) only the amount $CT$ invested with zero returns. The minus one term accounts for the staggered annuity of investments. In the limit $T\to \infty$ the formula reduces to $C/r(1+r)^T$ as if all investments had been made in a single lump on day one and invested for time $T$.

Front- and back-loading

Under the front-loaded strategy (pay NI first, invest later), the equation for the ETF pot at retirement is the same but only has $T-k$ total years to accumulate immediately before retirement:

Eq. 2 — Front-loaded ETF pot
$$A = \frac{C}{r}\left[(1+r)^{T-k} - 1\right]$$

You can confirm that when $k=T$, i.e. NI contributions constitute the entire window until retirement, the size of the ETF pot is zero.

Under the back-loaded strategy (invest first, pay NI last — pot untouched throughout), the ETF pot accumulates for the same $T-k$ years but then continues to compound for the remaining $k$ years leading up to retirement:

Eq. 3 — Back-loaded ETF pot
$$B = \frac{C}{r}\left[(1+r)^{T-k} - 1\right](1+r)^k = A(1+r)^k$$

There is already something important to observe. The ratio of the front-loaded to the back-loaded pot has a particularly simple form that is independent of $T$:

Eq. 4 — Core relationship I: Ratio of frontloading to backloading
$$A/B = (1+r)^{-k}$$

This shows that strategy B is equivalent to the final amount accumulated by strategy A, grown as a lump sum for a further $k$ years — regardless of the time horizon.

Backloading advantage

For practical considerations it is more common to work with the difference between these two pot sizes rather than their ratio. The exact advantage of back-loading over front-loading is:

Eq. 5 — Back minus front advantage
$$B - A = \frac{C}{r}\left[(1+r)^{T-k}-1\right]\left[(1+r)^k-1\right]$$

This expression is symmetric under $k \to T-k$, confirming that Chart 4 is exactly symmetric around $k = T/2$. The peak at $k^* = T/2$ follows from setting $\mathrm{d}(B-A)/\mathrm{d}k = 0$.

Note that if we normalize by B, we once again get a particularly simple form:

Eq. 6 — Relative back minus front advantage
$$\frac{B - A}{B} = 1 - \frac{A}{B} = 1 - (1+r)^{-k}$$

This shows the advantage as a fraction of the total return from the backloading strategy, which is again independent of $T$.

Asymptotic behaviour for $T \gg k$

Backloading Advantage

It is informative to study the behaviour for $T \gg k$ to understand the limiting exponential growth character. Starting with the $B-A$ advantage (Eq. 5), the limit implies $(1+r)^{T-k} \gg 1$ so we get the following approximation:

Eq. 7 — Asymptote of B - A
$$B - A|_{T \gg k} \approx \frac{C}{r}(1+r)^T\left[1-(1+r)^{-k}\right] = \frac{C}{r}(1+r)^T \cdot \frac{B-A}{B} $$

Here we have substituted the exact fractional advantage of backloading (Eq. 6) which is independent of $T$. In this limit, the advantage is the return that would come from making all investments as a lump sum at time zero — which is also limit for the Pure ETF investment for all $T$ years — scaled by this fractional advantage. The absolute advantage is exponential in $T$ with base $(1+r)$ with the exact curve approaching this asymptote from below. The asymptote exceeds the exact value by a constant value:

Eq. 8 — Gap to B − A asymptote
$$B - A\big|_{\text{asymp}} - (B-A) = \frac{C}{r}\left[(1+r)^k - 1\right]$$

This difference is equivalent to $k$ years of Pure ETF accumulation and is also independent of $T$. The exact $B−A$ and its asymptote therefore share the same exponential growth rate but differ by a constant, with their ratio tending to unity for $T \gg k$.

Backloading and Frontloading

Cancelling the exact factor $\frac{B-A}{B}$ from both sides of Eq. 7 immediately gives the asymptote for $B$:

Eq. 9 — Asymptote of B
$$\left.B\right|_{T \gg k} \approx \frac{C}{r}(1+r)^T$$

This is just the lump sum invested at time zero — the same as the limit for the pure ETF investment without NI contributions. To reiterate: the Pure ETF and Backloading strategy have the exact same asymptotic limits and the Pure ETF, Backloading strategy, and the Backloading Advantage all share the same exponent $T$.

Since we also know that $A = B(1+r)^{-k}$ exactly for all $T$ (Eq. 4), the asymptote for $A$ also follows immediately from the asymptote for $B$:

Eq. 10 — Asymptote of A
$$\left.A\right|_{T \gg k} \approx \frac{C}{r}(1+r)^T \cdot (1+r)^{-k} = \frac{C}{r}(1+r)^{T-k}$$

This has the same exponential form but with a different exponent, $T-k$. This means that backloading effectively has a head start of $k$ years over frontloading. Furthermore, since $A$ and $B$ have different exponents they have different slopes on a log plot. In the absolute sense $B-A$ grows exponentially with $T$, while in the relative sense the ratio $B/A = (1+r)^k$ is a constant for all $T$ — backloading therefore maintains a permanent multiplicative advantage corresponding to $k$ years of compounding.

The continuous uniform mixed strategy

We wish to explore different intermediate strategies that fall somewhere between full front- or backloading. If we study Chart 7, it is natural to plot the exact linear spline that separates front- and back-loaded strategies. This spline is given by a continuous uniform mixed strategy that invests a fraction $(1-k/T)$ of $C$ into the ETF each year:

Eq. 11 — Uniform mixed strategy (continuous)
$$M = \frac{T-k}{T}\frac{C}{r}\left[(1+r)^T - 1\right]$$

This is linear in $k$, confirming that the uniform mixed strategy traces the straight line between the $k=0$ and $k=T$ endpoints in Chart 7 — interpolating linearly between the pure ETF return and the return on zero investments.

Normalization by the Pure ETF pot

Normalization by the Pure ETF investment Eq. 1 produces a particularly simple form which is independent of the return $r$:

Eq. 12 — Core relationship II: the uniform strategy
$$\tilde{M} = \frac{M}{\text{Pure ETF}} = 1 - \frac{k}{T}$$

This shows that the uniform mixed strategy always captures exactly the fraction $(1 - k/T)$ of the pure ETF pot, equal to the fraction of years spent investing rather than paying NI. The compounding structure cancels completely. This means $\tilde{M}$ is the only normalised quantity in the model that is independent of $r$: it is anchored to a purely geometric property of the time allocation.

The continuous uniform mixed strategy is the natural uniform midpoint between front and back-loading. However, it can only be approximated using whole-sum, as opposed to fractional, NI contributions. Below we look at strategies to approximate $M$ using a whole-sum schedule, which leads naturally to exploring different families of strategies that fall somewhere between full front- and back-loading.

Other normalized quantities

For convenience we also provide expressions for A, B and B-A normalized by the Pure ETF:

Eq. 13 — ETF Normalized Frontloading
$$\tilde{A} = \frac{A}{\text{Pure ETF}} = \frac{(1+r)^{T-k} - 1}{(1+r)^T - 1}$$
Eq. 14 — ETF Normalized Backloading
$$\tilde{B} = \frac{B}{\text{Pure ETF}} = \frac{\left[(1+r)^{T-k} - 1\right](1+r)^k}{(1+r)^T - 1}$$
Eq. 15 — ETF Normalized B - A
$$\tilde{B} - \tilde{A} = \frac{B-A}{\text{Pure ETF}} = \frac{(1+r)^{T-k} - 1}{(1+r)^T - 1}\left[(1+r)^k - 1\right]$$

These forms are used to plot $\tilde{A}$, $\tilde{B}$, the advantage $\tilde{B} - \tilde{A}$, and $\tilde{M}$ vs $k/T$ on shared dimensionless axes — see Chart 7.

Generalised schedules

Having established that back-loading dominates front-loading and studied the risks that might push in the other direction, we now want to move beyond the two extreme strategies. The goal is a family of schedules that interpolates smoothly between full front- and back-loading, passing through the uniform strategy at the midpoint, so that a reader can choose a position that matches their personal risk tolerance.

The starting point is simple: if the $k$ payment years were spaced uniformly across $[1,T]$, the schedule would be perfectly neutral. To favour back-loading we want to compress those evenly spaced points toward year $T$; to favour front-loading we want to compress them toward year 1. The question is: what mathematical function should do the compressing?

It turns out that power law scaling, $x \mapsto x^{p}$, is the natural choice: it maps $[0,1]$ exactly onto $[0,1]$ for any $p > 0$. At $p=1$ the mapping is the identity — uniform spacing preserved. As $p$ decreases below 1, points are compressed toward 1 (back-loading); as $p$ increases above 1 they are compressed toward 0 (front-loading). After rescaling from $[0,1]$ to $[1,T]$ this gives the power law schedule. Its mirror image — compressing in the opposite direction via $u \mapsto 1-(1-u)^{p}$ — gives the beta quantile schedule, where small $p$ now means front-loading and large $p$ means back-loading. Together they form two related one-parameter families that smoothly interpolate between the scheduling extremes.

To use them we first determine a continuous position for the $i$-th NI payment:

Eq. 16 — Power law schedule $\equiv \mathrm{Beta}(p,1)$ quantile
$$y_i^{\text{pow}} = 1 + (T-1) \cdot u_i^{p}$$
Eq. 17 — Beta quantile schedule $\equiv \mathrm{Beta}(1,p)$ quantile
$$y_i^{\text{beta}} = 1 + (T-1) \cdot \left[1 - \left(1 - u_i\right)^{p}\right]$$
Eq. 18 — Normalised index (midpoint rule)
$$u_i = \frac{i + \tfrac{1}{2}}{k} = \frac{2i+1}{2k}, \qquad i \in \{0, \ldots, k-1\}$$

Here, the shift and rescaling by $(T-1)$ maps continuous positions from $[0,1]$ into $[1, T]$ with the schedules parameterized fully by the single shape parameter $p$ — more on the form of $u_i$ later. All that remains is to map the $k$ continuous positions onto discrete contribution years within the $T$-year window.

Integer assignment

Continuous positions $y_i$ are converted to distinct integer payment years in a single forward pass. For each payment in order from $i=0$ to $k-1$, the algorithm assigns the nearest available integer subject to two constraints: a lower bound that prevents collision with the previous assignment, and an upper bound that leaves enough remaining integers for subsequent payments:

Eq. 19 — Forward pass assignment rule
$$a_i = \max\!\bigl(a_{i-1}+1,\;\min\!\bigl(T-k+i+1,\;\mathrm{round}(y_i)\bigr)\bigr)$$

The lower bound $a_{i-1}+1$ prevents collision with the previous payment. The upper bound $(T-k+i+1)$ ensures that there remain enough integer years after position $i$ for the $k-(i+1)$ later payments. The two constraints together guarantee a strictly increasing assignment of distinct integers in $[1,T]$. The figure below shows a worked example: the beta quantile schedule at $p=5$ (strongly back-loaded), $k=6$, $T=20$ produces continuous positions that crowd heavily near year $T$, with naive rounding placing three payments at year 20; the forward pass resolves the collisions, distributing payments to years 8, 15, 17, 18, 19, and 20.

Relationship between the power law and beta quantile schedules

The two schedules $y_i^{\text{pow}}$ and $y_i^{\text{beta}}$ are algebraically related by a reflection through the centre of the unit square: $u \to 1-u$ with the same $p$. This is because $u^{p}$ reflected through $(0.5, 0.5)$ gives $1-(1-u)^{p}$, which is exactly the beta quantile formula at the same $p$. Both reduce to the same uniform schedule at $p=1$. The left and right panels of the figure below show this directly: each coloured curve in the left panel corresponds to its exact reflection at the same $p$ through the point $(0.5, 0.5)$ in the right panel.

Both forms handle front-loading (small $p$ for power law, large $p$ for beta quantile) and back-loading (large $p$ for power law, small $p$ for beta quantile) within a single continuous family. As a consequence of the reflection relationship, the two schedules are asymmetric in the same way: for a given $p$, the degree of compression toward one end is the mirror image of the other's. For full symmetry across both ends of the range, one may therefore prefer the power law for front-loading and the beta quantile for back-loading, or vice versa.

Probabilistic interpretation

The power law and beta quantile schedules have a natural probabilistic interpretation in terms of the Beta distribution — a family of distributions with support exactly on $[0,1]$, which maps directly onto the scheduling window $[1,T]$ after rescaling.

Specifically:

Power law schedule at $p$  $\equiv$  quantile function of $\mathrm{Beta}(p,\; 1)$

Beta quantile schedule at $p$  $\equiv$  quantile function of $\mathrm{Beta}(1,\; p)$

The two families are the unique one-parameter subfamilies of the Beta family with monotone densities — $\mathrm{Beta}(p,1)$ is always monotone decreasing for $p < 1$ and increasing for $p > 1$, and vice versa for $\mathrm{Beta}(1,p)$ — making them the natural minimal models for a pure single-parameter scheduling preference. However, they can only represent a preference for one end of the window — either front- or back-loading — and not both simultaneously. For a more complex risk profile we need to consider the full $\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ family.

Consider, for instance, that we might encounter the following scenario:

Pension side risk (favours deferal): we avoid making contributions in case the pension scheme deteriorates — set $\beta \lt 1$ to place weight near year $T$.

Contribution side risk (favours early payment): pay while you can afford to and before any potential real term contribution fee rise — set $\alpha \lt 1$ to place weight near year $1$.

A person who fears both circumstances and is uncertain about the timing of either naturally arrives at a U-shaped density with both $\alpha < 1$ and $\beta < 1$: sparse in the middle years, which provide the weakest hedge against either risk, and dense at both ends where the hedges are strongest.

With both parameters above 1 the density is hump-shaped, concentrating payments in the interior years. This might be appropirate for someone who fears aggressively back-loading in case the number of contributing years to qualify for a full pension is raised and who expects to have greatest financial liquidity in their middle years.

We see, therefore, that the general Beta family can represent a wide range of risk profiles to match different circumstances. This will be covered in more detail in a follow-up arcitle, along with a further refinement — combining the risk prior with a uniform distribution over the back-loading window $[1-k/T, 1]$ to encode the back-loading optimum as the neutral zero-risk baseline.

End-point pinning and the midpoint rule

The naive starting point for a normalised index is $u_i = i/(k-1)$ for $i \in \{0, \ldots, k-1\}$. This maps $k$ equally spaced points exactly onto $[0,1]$ with no exterior margins: $u_0 = 0$ and $u_{k-1} = 1$, with uniform spacing $1/(k-1)$ between them. At $p=1$ the schedule is perfectly evenly spread from year 1 to year $T$ and includes both end points.

The problem is that $u_0 = 0$ and $u_{k-1} = 1$ exactly, so:

  • $y_0 = 1 + (T-1)\cdot 0^{p} = 1$ for all $p > 0$ — first payment always pinned at year 1
  • $y_{k-1} = 1 + (T-1)\cdot 1^{p} = T$ for all $p$ — last payment always pinned at year T

Both endpoints are pinned for every value of $p$, including for aggressively front- and back-loaded parametrizations.

p = 0.25  (back-loaded) p = 1  (uniform) p = 2 p = 4  (front-loaded)

Left: natural grid — all four curves share the same endpoints (year 1 and year 20) regardless of $p$, since both boundaries are pinned. Right: midpoint rule — both boundaries are free. The curves fan out naturally as $p$ varies, with back-loaded schedules clustering near year 20 and front-loaded schedules clustering near year 1, but neither boundary is forced.

The natural solution is to use the midpoint rule: $$u_i = \frac{i + \tfrac{1}{2}}{k} = \frac{2i+1}{2k}, \qquad i \in \{0, \ldots, k-1\}$$ This unpins both ends and has a probabilistic interpretation — the $u_i$ are the midpoints of the $k$ quantiles in the underlying probability distribution. It does however necessarily leave an unutilized margin, which is largest for the uniform schedule $p=1$: for values $T=31$ and $k=15$ years the margin is 2 years on either side, which is not a negligible number of years to forfeit. If desired one can always use a nonlinear function of $i$ or an additional free parameter to reduce the boundary offset. However, the mathematical simplicity and probabilistic interpretation of the midpoint formula are convenient for our present and future needs.
